Formative Activity – Reasoning Quiz

This formative activity focuses on distinguishing between deductive and inductive reasoning. Below, I present each question, the correct classification, my answer, and a brief explanation of the reasoning involved.

Deductive vs. Inductive Arguments

Questions and reasoning

Each card summarises the question, the correct type of reasoning, my answer, and a short explanation of why it is deductive or inductive.

Deduction

Question 1 Correct

Statement: If A = B and B = C, then A = C.

My answer: Deduction (correct)

This is a classical logical rule. The conclusion follows necessarily from the premises with no uncertainty, so it is a clear example of deductive reasoning.

Deduction

Question 2 Correct

Statement: Since all squares are rectangles, and all rectangles have four sides, all squares have four sides.

My answer: Deduction (correct)

The argument applies general definitions (square → rectangle, rectangle → four sides) to derive a necessary conclusion about squares. Because the conclusion must be true if the premises are true, this is deductive reasoning.

Deduction

Question 3 Correct

Statement: All cats have a keen sense of smell. Fluffy is a cat, so Fluffy has a keen sense of smell.

My answer: Deduction (correct)

A general rule about all cats is applied to one specific cat (Fluffy). Given the premise, the conclusion follows with certainty, so this is a deductive argument.

Induction Learning point

Question 4 Incorrect originally

Statement: Every time you eat peanuts, your throat swells and you can’t breathe. This is a symptom of people who are allergic to peanuts. Therefore, you are allergic to peanuts.

My original answer: Deduction (incorrect)
Correct classification: Induction

The conclusion is based on repeated observations of a pattern (whenever peanuts are eaten, swelling occurs) and on a medical symptom. It generalises from experience to a probable conclusion, rather than applying a strict universal rule. For this reason, the argument is inductive rather than deductive.

What I learned: even when the evidence is strong, reasoning is still inductive if it relies on patterns and observations rather than on formal logical rules.

Deduction

Question 5 Correct

Statement: Tony is a football player. All football players weigh more than 170 pounds. Therefore, Tony weighs more than 170 pounds.

My answer: Deduction (correct)

A general statement about all football players is applied directly to Tony. If the premise is accepted, the conclusion must be true, so this is an example of deductive reasoning.

Induction

Question 6 Correct

Statement: All cars in this town drive on the right side of the street. Therefore, all cars in all towns drive on the right side of the street.

My answer: Induction (correct)

The argument overgeneralises from a local observation (one town) to a universal claim (all towns). It extends limited evidence to a broad conclusion, which is typical of inductive reasoning.

Reflection

A brief reflection on what I learned from this activity.

This formative quiz helped me sharpen the distinction between deductive and inductive reasoning. Deductive arguments produce conclusions that follow necessarily from their premises, while inductive arguments move from patterns and observations to probable conclusions.

Question 4 was especially valuable as a learning point. Although the evidence was strong, the reasoning was still inductive because it relied on repeated experience rather than a formal rule that applies to everyone. This insight will support my future work in knowledge representation and logical argumentation, where correctly classifying the type of reasoning is essential.
