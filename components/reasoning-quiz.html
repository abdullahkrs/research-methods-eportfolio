Formative Activity: Reasoning Quiz

Component: Formative Activity – Logical Reasoning

Focus: Distinguishing deductive and inductive arguments

This formative quiz assessed my ability to classify arguments as deductive or inductive and explain the underlying reasoning.

Questions and reasoning

Each card below summarises the question, the correct classification (deductive or inductive), and a brief explanation of the reasoning.

Question 1
Deduction

If A = B and B = C, then A = C.

My answer: Deduction (correct)

This is a classical logical rule: the conclusion follows necessarily from the premises. There is no uncertainty, so it is an example of deductive reasoning.

Question 2
Deduction

Since all squares are rectangles, and all rectangles have four sides, all squares have four sides.

My answer: Deduction (correct)

A general set of definitions is applied directly to squares. Given the premises, the conclusion must be true; therefore, this is deductive reasoning.

Question 3
Deduction

All cats have a keen sense of smell. Fluffy is a cat, so Fluffy has a keen sense of smell.

My answer: Deduction (correct)

The argument applies a general rule about all cats to a specific instance (Fluffy). The conclusion follows with certainty, which makes this a deductive argument.

Question 4 – Learning point
Induction

Every time you eat peanuts, your throat swells and you can’t breathe. This is a symptom of people who are allergic to peanuts. Therefore, you are allergic to peanuts.

My original answer: Deduction (incorrect)
Correct classification: Induction

The conclusion is based on repeated observations of a pattern and a medical symptom, rather than on a universal rule that applies to all people. It generalises from experience to a probable conclusion, so it is inductive reasoning rather than deductive. I learned that even very strong evidence can still be inductive if it relies on patterns instead of strict logical rules.

Question 5
Deduction

Tony is a football player. All football players weigh more than 170 pounds. Therefore, Tony weighs more than 170 pounds.

My answer: Deduction (correct)

A general statement about all football players is applied directly to Tony. If the premise is accepted, the conclusion must be true, which makes this a deductive argument.

Question 6
Induction

All cars in this town drive on the right side of the street. Therefore, all cars in all towns drive on the right side of the street.

My answer: Induction (correct)

The argument overgeneralises from one town to every town. It extends a limited observation to a broad conclusion, which is typical of inductive reasoning.

Reflection

This formative activity sharpened my ability to distinguish between deductive and inductive arguments. I confirmed that deductive reasoning produces conclusions that follow necessarily from their premises, while inductive reasoning draws probable conclusions from patterns and observations.

Question 4 was particularly useful: although the evidence was strong, the conclusion was still inductive because it relied on repeated experience rather than a formal rule. This insight will help me build stronger, better justified arguments in future work on knowledge representation and logical reasoning.
