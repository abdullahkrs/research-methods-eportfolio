Seminar 2 – Activity

Research Methods Comparison for Two AI Papers

This page presents my preparation for Seminar 2, where I analysed two Computing papers on Artificial Intelligence that use different research methods. The answers are structured into separate tables based on the seminar questions.

I selected two recent AI-related papers in Computing. The first uses a quantitative bibliometric approach to map research trends in AI and management. The second uses a mixed-method approach to examine the impact of AI and machine learning on social and economic progress. Below, each seminar question is answered in its own comparison table to keep the analysis clear and easy to follow.

1. Purpose, Problem, Objective & Alignment with My Experience

This table summarises the purpose and objectives of each paper and explains how they relate to my own experience and thoughts on AI in Computing.

Table 1. Purpose, Problem, Objective and Alignment
Paper Purpose / Problem / Objective Alignment with My Experience / Thoughts
Paper 1: Artificial Intelligence in Management Studies (2021–2025):
A Bibliometric Mapping of Themes, Trends, and Global Contributions 		The paper aims to map research on Artificial Intelligence in management from 2021 to 2025. It identifies key themes, trends, leading countries, authors and keyword clusters using bibliometric indicators. The main objective is to describe how AI-related management research has evolved and which topics dominate the field. This aligns with my experience because I work with AI in real organisational and public-sector contexts. Understanding global research trends helps me see how my own AI work (e.g. decision-support systems, municipal AI projects) fits into the broader research landscape and where gaps or opportunities may exist.
Paper 2: Prospects and Challenges of the Impact of Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning on Social and Economic Progress The purpose of this paper is to examine how AI and machine learning influence economic development and social structures. It discusses opportunities and risks, and empirically explores the relationship between AI readiness levels and economic indicators such as GDP. This connects strongly with my interests because I am involved in AI projects that affect public services, digital transformation and policy. The focus on AI readiness, economic impact and social implications matches my thinking about how AI should be adopted responsibly at national and organisational levels.

2. Appropriateness of the Research Methodology

The following table evaluates whether the research methodology used in each paper is suitable for the stated purpose or research question, based on the definitions of qualitative, quantitative and mixed methods introduced in the seminars.

Table 2. Research Methodology and Its Appropriateness
Paper Research Method Used Is the Methodology Appropriate? (Justification)
Paper 1 The study uses a quantitative bibliometric method. It collects publication data from a bibliographic database (such as Scopus), and applies bibliometric tools to count documents, analyse citations, and generate maps of themes, trends and collaborations. Yes, the methodology is appropriate. The research question is descriptive and focuses on “what are the themes, trends and global contributions?”. This is best answered using numerical evidence about publications and keywords. Bibliometric analysis is the standard quantitative approach for mapping research fields, so it fits the purpose very well.
Paper 2 The paper uses a mixed-method approach. It combines a qualitative literature review (to understand theoretical perspectives, opportunities and challenges) with quantitative statistical analysis (e.g. correlation between AI readiness indices and GDP). Yes, the methodology is appropriate. The topic involves both conceptual issues (ethics, social impact, economic implications) and measurable relationships (AI readiness and economic indicators). Mixed methods are well suited to this, as they allow the authors to link theory and empirical data. A single method would not capture the topic as completely.

3. Data Collection and Analysis – Are They Appropriate?

This table reviews what kinds of data each paper uses, how the data are analysed, and whether these choices are suitable for answering the research questions.

Table 3. Appropriateness of Data Collection and Analysis
Paper Data Collection Data Analysis Appropriateness for the Purpose / Question
Paper 1 The authors rely on secondary data from a bibliographic database. They filter AI-related management publications by year (2021–2025), subject area and other criteria to build the dataset used for analysis. They apply bibliometric techniques and visualisation tools to examine publication counts per year, citation patterns, keyword co-occurrence and co-authorship networks. The results are presented in tables and maps. This is appropriate because the aim is to describe how the literature is structured, not to collect new primary data. Bibliographic databases and bibliometric analysis are suitable for studying large bodies of research and identifying patterns across them.
Paper 2 The paper uses secondary data such as AI readiness indices, economic indicators (e.g. GDP) and published literature (research articles, reports, policy documents) to frame the discussion. The authors employ statistical techniques (for example, correlation analysis) to explore the relationship between AI readiness and GDP, and they interpret these findings using insights from the literature review. This combination is suitable for the research aims. Correlation analysis is an appropriate way to examine associations between quantitative variables, while the literature review helps to interpret what these relationships might mean socially and economically. The approach also recognises that correlation shows association rather than causation.

4. Support for Claims and Conclusions

The next table considers whether each paper supports its claims and conclusions with explicit arguments and evidence.

Table 4. Support for Claims and Conclusions
Paper Does the Paper Support Its Claims? Type of Evidence / Argumentation
Paper 1 Yes. The conclusions about research growth, dominant themes and leading contributors are backed by clear quantitative results. The paper presents numerical tables, charts and visual maps showing publication trends, keyword clusters and citation relationships. The authors then interpret these results and link them back to the aims of the study, explaining what the patterns suggest about AI in management.
Paper 2 Yes. The conclusions about the impact of AI and ML on social and economic progress are supported through a combination of empirical findings and theoretical reasoning. The paper provides correlation results between AI readiness and GDP, and complements these with discussions drawn from prior research on productivity, innovation, inequality and labour market effects. This mix of data and theory offers explicit support for the authors’ claims, while also acknowledging challenges and risks.

5. How I Would Enhance Each Paper

Finally, this table outlines how I would enhance or extend each paper, based on my understanding of research methods and good academic practice.

Table 5. Suggested Enhancements to the Papers
Paper Suggested Enhancements
Paper 1
  • Add qualitative insights (for example, expert interviews) to help explain why certain themes, countries or authors dominate.
  • Include a clearer discussion of limitations, such as database coverage, language filters and document-type restrictions.
  • Extend the analysis with simple forecasting to show possible future trends in AI management research.
  • Provide stronger practical implications for managers, explaining how they might use the mapped themes to guide AI strategy.
Paper 2
  • Move beyond simple correlation by using regression or models that include additional variables (e.g. education level, R&D spending, digital infrastructure).
  • Add case studies of specific countries or sectors to illustrate how AI readiness translates into real socio-economic outcomes.
  • Incorporate primary data, such as interviews or surveys with policymakers or practitioners, to validate and enrich the secondary data.
  • Strengthen the limitations section, especially on the use of secondary indicators and the fact that correlation does not prove causation.

These enhancements are based on principles covered in the seminars, including the value of mixed evidence, transparent limitations, and ensuring strong alignment between research questions, methods and conclusions.
