Seminar 2 – Peer Review Activity

Methodology and Research Methods – Peer Review

This page documents my contribution to the Seminar 2 peer review activity, including my own research outline, the outline I reviewed, the feedback exchanged, and how this supported my understanding of research design.

1. Overview of the Activity

The table below summarises the purpose of the Seminar 2 Peer Review Activity and my role within it.

Table 1. Activity Overview
Seminar focus Unit 3 – Methodology and Research Methods: connecting research questions, research design, and the choice of qualitative, quantitative or mixed methods.
Main task Each student prepared a short research outline, then peer-reviewed another student’s outline, focusing on the alignment between research questions, methods, and data collection.
My contributions (1) Drafted my own outline for an AI-related computing topic.
(2) Reviewed a peer’s outline using Seminar 2 concepts.
(3) Reflected on feedback I gave and received.
Key concepts applied Research purpose and questions; exploratory vs conclusive design; qualitative vs quantitative vs mixed methods; appropriateness of methods and data; ethical and feasibility considerations.

2. My Research Outline

This table summarises the core elements of my own research outline prepared for Seminar 2.

Table 2. Summary of My Research Outline
Topic area Artificial Intelligence within Computing (e.g. intelligent systems for decision support such as fraud detection, intelligent agents, or geospatial analysis).
Research problem There is a need to understand how AI techniques can be applied in a specific computing context to improve decision-making, performance, or user experience, while remaining feasible and ethical.
Research aim To investigate how a selected AI approach can be designed, implemented and evaluated in a defined scenario (for example, detecting anomalies, supporting municipal decisions, or classifying transactions).
Research question(s) Example: “How effectively can [chosen AI technique] improve [specific computing task] compared with a baseline approach?” The question is designed to be clear, focused and answerable within the module.
Proposed method A [quantitative / qualitative / mixed] approach (to be finalised), chosen to match the nature of the data and the type of question. For instance, a quantitative experiment for performance comparison or a qualitative study for exploring user perceptions.
Data collection Depending on the final design: structured datasets, system logs, survey responses, or interview transcripts. Emphasis on accessible, ethical and realistic data sources.
Data analysis For quantitative designs: descriptive statistics and performance metrics (e.g. accuracy, precision, recall).
For qualitative designs: coding and thematic analysis to identify recurring patterns or themes.
Ethical / practical considerations Ensuring data privacy, anonymisation where required, and realistic scope within the module timeframe. Seeking ethical approval where human participants are involved.

3. Peer’s Outline Reviewed

The following table summarises the main elements of the peer outline I reviewed (kept anonymous).

Table 3. Summary of Peer’s Research Outline
Topic area A computing topic such as cybersecurity, AI in healthcare, or data science in education, addressing a practical issue like security awareness, prediction, or user behaviour.
Research problem The peer highlighted a gap or challenge (for example, limited understanding of user behaviour or insufficient evaluation of a specific tool).
Stated aim To examine or improve a specific aspect of the chosen domain, such as user security practices, performance of a predictive model, or adoption of a technology.
Research question(s) The peer provided one or more questions, for example “What factors influence X?” or “To what extent does Y improve Z?”. My role was to check clarity and alignment with the methods.
Chosen method Typically either a survey-based quantitative study or a qualitative case study, depending on whether the peer wanted to measure frequency/relationships or explore experiences in depth.
Data collection and analysis The peer outlined planned data sources such as questionnaires, interviews, or log data, and named basic analysis approaches (e.g. descriptive statistics or thematic analysis).

4. Feedback I Provided to My Peer

The table below captures the main feedback points I gave during the peer review.

Table 4. Key Feedback I Provided
Aspect Reviewed My Feedback
Clarity of research question I encouraged the peer to make the question more specific (who, what, where) and to ensure that the scope was manageable within the module timeline.
Alignment between question and method I checked whether the chosen method matched the type of question. For “how many / how much” questions, I supported quantitative approaches; for “how / why” questions, I suggested qualitative methods where appropriate.
Data collection strategy I asked whether the proposed data sources were realistic, accessible and sufficient to answer the research question, and suggested clarifying sampling and recruitment.
Analysis plan I recommended clearly stating how the data would be analysed (e.g. specific statistics or thematic coding) to make the plan more systematic and convincing.
Ethical considerations I reminded the peer to consider consent, anonymity and data protection, especially when collecting data from human participants.
Tone and constructiveness I aimed to balance critique with positive comments, acknowledging strengths in the outline while suggesting concrete improvements.

5. Feedback I Received on My Outline

This table summarises the most important feedback I received from my peer and tutor.

Table 5. Key Feedback I Received
Feedback Theme Details
Focus and scope I was advised to narrow the topic and define the context more clearly so that the study would be achievable within the time and resource constraints.
Justification of method The feedback highlighted the need to explain more explicitly why my chosen method was the most suitable for my research question, referring to the concepts of qualitative, quantitative and mixed methods from the unit.
Data and feasibility I was encouraged to think carefully about how I would access the data, the number of participants (if any), and how much analysis would be realistic.
Ethical awareness The tutor reminded me that even small projects require ethical thinking, including consent and confidentiality when using human data or sensitive information.
Overall structure I was praised for having a clear idea but encouraged to make the logical connection between problem, question, method and data more explicit and structured.

6. Summary Reflection on the Activity

The following table captures my main learning points from Seminar 2.

Table 6. Reflection on Learning from Peer Review
Reflection Area What I Learned
Applying theory to practice Reviewing a peer’s outline helped me actively apply the concepts of research design, method choice and data alignment, rather than just reading about them.
Critical evaluation skills I improved my ability to identify strengths and weaknesses in research plans and to provide feedback that is specific, fair and grounded in the unit content.
Receiving feedback The comments I received showed me that even a promising idea needs a clear and well-justified methodology. This has influenced how I plan future assignments.
Ethics and feasibility I now pay more attention to what is realistic within a project and how ethical issues should be considered from the beginning, not at the end.
Future practice I intend to use a similar peer review mindset when planning later projects: checking alignment between problem, question, design, method and data before starting any implementation.
