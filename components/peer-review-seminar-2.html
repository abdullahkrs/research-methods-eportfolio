Research Methods Comparison for Two AI Papers

The analysis is organised into separate tables following the seminar questions on purpose, methodology, data collection and analysis, evidence, and possible enhancements.

2. Appropriateness of the Research Methodology

The table below evaluates whether the chosen research methodology in each paper is suitable for the stated purpose or research question.

Table 2. Research Methodology and Its Appropriateness
Paper Research Method Used Is the Methodology Appropriate? (Justification)
Paper 1 The paper uses a quantitative bibliometric method. It collects publication data from a database (e.g. Scopus), counts documents, citations and keywords, and applies bibliometric tools to generate maps of themes, trends and collaborations. Yes, this methodology is appropriate. The research question focuses on “what are the themes and trends” in the literature, which is essentially a descriptive and quantitative question. Bibliometric analysis is the standard way to measure research output and thematic patterns, so it matches the purpose well.
Paper 2 The paper uses a mixed-method approach. It combines a qualitative literature review (to identify theoretical perspectives, opportunities and challenges) with quantitative statistical analysis (e.g. correlation between AI readiness scores and GDP). Yes, this methodology is appropriate. The topic concerns both conceptual issues (social and economic implications) and measurable relationships (AI readiness and economic performance). Mixed methods provide a broader and deeper view than a single method and align with the paper’s aim of capturing both theory and empirical evidence.

3. Data Collection and Analysis – Are They Appropriate?

This table reviews the types of data used in each paper and the analysis techniques, and evaluates whether they are suitable for answering the research questions.

Table 3. Appropriateness of Data Collection and Analysis
Paper Data Collection Data Analysis Appropriateness for the Purpose / Question
Paper 1 Uses secondary data from a bibliographic database, filtered by year, subject area and other criteria. The dataset consists of AI-related management publications from 2021–2025. Applies bibliometric analysis and visualisation tools (e.g. counting publications per year, analysing citations, mapping co-occurring keywords and co-authorship networks). This is appropriate because the aim is to describe patterns in the existing literature, not to collect new primary data. The combination of database filtering and bibliometric statistics is well suited to mapping research themes and trends at scale.
Paper 2 Uses secondary data such as AI readiness indices and economic indicators like GDP, along with published research and policy documents to provide context. Employs statistical analysis (for example, Pearson correlation) to explore the relationship between AI readiness and economic performance, supported by qualitative interpretation from the literature review. This combination is suitable for the research aims. Correlation analysis is appropriate for studying how two measurable variables are related, while the literature review helps interpret the results and situate them in a broader social and economic context. It also respects the limits of correlation (showing association, not causation).

5. How I Would Enhance Each Paper

Finally, this table lists my suggestions for improving or extending the work in each paper.

Table 5. Suggested Enhancements to the Papers
Paper Suggested Enhancements
Paper 1
  • Add qualitative insights, such as expert interviews, to explain why certain themes and countries dominate.
  • Discuss limitations more explicitly, e.g. database coverage, language restrictions or document-type filters.
  • Include forward-looking analysis, such as predicting future trends in AI management research.
  • Provide clearer managerial implications, linking the mapped themes to practical decision-making in organisations.
Paper 2
  • Move beyond simple correlation by using regression or models that include more variables (education, R&D, digital infrastructure).
  • Incorporate case studies of specific countries or sectors to illustrate how AI readiness translates into real outcomes.
  • Add primary data (e.g. interviews or surveys with policymakers or practitioners) to validate and enrich the quantitative findings.
  • Strengthen the limitations section, especially around the use of secondary data and the fact that correlation does not prove causation.

These enhancements are based on the research-methods principles from the module, including the importance of triangulation, clear limitation statements, and alignment between questions, methods and evidence.
