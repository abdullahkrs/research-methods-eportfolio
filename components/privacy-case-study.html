Case Study on Privacy – Access to Property Tax Records

This e-Portfolio component analyses a privacy case involving a records clerk, a researcher, and access to government-held property tax data, focusing on ethical issues such as confidentiality, consent, and professional responsibility.

Privacy Data Protection Research Ethics Professional Conduct

The Case

Title: Case Study on Privacy

Ricardo works for the records department of his local government as a computer records clerk, where he has access to files of property tax records. For a scientific study, a researcher, Beth, has been granted access to the numerical portion “but not the corresponding names” of some records.

Beth finds some information that she would like to use, but she needs the names and addresses corresponding with certain properties. Beth asks Ricardo to retrieve these names and addresses, so she can contact these people for more information and for permission to do further study.

The following questions explore the ethical issues involved in deciding how Ricardo, Beth, and the Records department should act.

Question 1

If Ricardo is not responsible for determining allowable access, should he release the names and addresses?

Question:

If Ricardo is not responsible for determining allowable access, should he release the names and addresses to Beth?

Short answer:

No. If Ricardo is not authorised to decide access, he should not release the names and addresses.

Ethical issues involved:

  • Confidentiality: Ricardo has a duty to protect the confidentiality of personal data held by the records department. Disclosing names and addresses without authorisation is a violation of this duty.
  • Role responsibility: His job as a records clerk requires him to follow formal policies and procedures for data access. Acting outside those procedures undermines institutional controls.
  • Legal and policy compliance: Government records are typically protected by data protection laws and internal regulations. Ricardo is obligated to comply with these, even if the requester is a legitimate researcher.
  • Trust in public institutions: Citizens expect that personal data given to the government will not be disclosed arbitrarily. Ricardo’s unauthorised disclosure could damage public trust in the records department.

Ethically, Ricardo should decline the request and refer Beth to the appropriate authority or data protection officer who is formally responsible for deciding access.

Question 2

What if Ricardo is responsible for determining allowable access?

Question:

Suppose Ricardo were responsible for determining allowable access to the files. What ethical issues would be involved in his deciding whether to grant access to Beth?

Key ethical issues:

  • Privacy vs. research benefit: Ricardo must balance individuals’ right to privacy against the potential social or scientific value of Beth’s research. Even worthwhile research does not automatically justify releasing identifiable data.
  • Informed consent: The individuals did not originally consent to have their data used for research. Ricardo must consider whether contacting them via information held for tax purposes is appropriate without prior consent.
  • Purpose limitation: Property tax data was collected for administrative reasons, not for research. Using that data for a new purpose without safeguards may violate the principle of using data only for its original, stated purpose, unless a clear legal and ethical basis exists.
  • Data minimisation: Ricardo should ask whether Beth truly needs names and addresses, or whether anonymised or aggregated data could achieve the research aims with less privacy risk.
  • Risk of harm and misuse: Once Beth receives identifiable data, there is a risk it could be misused, mishandled, or shared beyond the original study. Ricardo has a duty to consider these risks before granting access.
  • Impartiality and fairness: Decisions should be based on clear criteria and policy, not on personal sympathy for Beth or the attractiveness of the project.

In this scenario, Ricardo’s ethical responsibility is to apply institutional policy consistently, minimise risk to individuals, and ensure that any release of data is properly justified, documented, and subject to appropriate oversight.

Question 3

Should Beth be allowed to contact the individuals involved?

Question:

Should Beth be allowed to contact the individuals involved? That is, should the Records department release individuals’ names to a researcher? What are the ethical issues for the Records department to consider?

Ethical issues for the Records department:

  • Respect for privacy and autonomy: Individuals have a right to control who uses their personal data and for what purpose. Releasing names and addresses without their consent may infringe this right.
  • Purpose limitation and trust: The data was collected for tax administration. Reusing it to enable research contact can feel like a “function creep”, risking loss of trust in how government uses personal information.
  • Controlled and proportional access: If contact is permitted at all, it may be more ethical for the Records department to contact individuals on Beth’s behalf (e.g. via an official letter) rather than handing over the raw contact details.
  • Ethical review and oversight: The department should consider whether Beth has appropriate approval from an ethics committee or institutional review board, and whether data protection requirements are met.
  • Security and accountability: Once data leaves the department, ensuring proper storage, security, and eventual deletion becomes more difficult. Clear agreements and accountability mechanisms are needed.

In summary, the department should be extremely cautious about releasing identifiable information. If contact is allowed, it should follow a transparent process, with ethical review and safeguards that respect individuals’ privacy and autonomy.

Question 4

What options are available to Beth if responses are mixed?

Question:

Suppose Beth contacts the individuals to ask their permission, and one-third of them respond giving permission, one-third respond denying permission, and one-third do not respond. Beth claims that at least one-half of the individuals are needed to make a valid study. What options are available to Beth?

Ethically acceptable options:

  • Use only data from those who gave explicit permission: This respects autonomy and informed consent, although it may reduce the sample size below her ideal threshold.
  • Send a follow-up request (through appropriate channels): A second, clearly worded reminder—possibly sent by the Records department or via an official channel—may improve the response rate without pressuring individuals.
  • Revise the research design: Beth could adjust her methodology, refine her research questions, or use anonymised/aggregated data so that she can work ethically with a smaller or differently structured sample.
  • Consult an ethics committee: She may seek guidance on whether any form of non-response can be used in a limited, anonymised way, or whether the study should be redesigned or scaled back.

Options that are not ethically acceptable:

  • Treating non-response as implied consent and including those individuals in the study.
  • Reusing identifiable data from people who explicitly denied permission.
  • Pressuring individuals who refused, or repeatedly contacting non-respondents in a way that becomes intrusive.

The key principle is that silence does not equal consent. Beth should prioritise respect for individual decisions, even if that means compromising on her original sample size or adapting her research design.

Summary of Key Ethical Principles

This case highlights how everyday decisions about data access and research can raise fundamental questions of privacy, consent, and professional duty.

  • Confidentiality: Staff like Ricardo must protect confidential information and only disclose it in line with formal policies and legal requirements.
  • Informed consent: Individuals should have control over whether their personal data is used for research, and non-response cannot be treated as consent.
  • Purpose limitation: Data collected for one purpose (e.g. taxation) should not be repurposed for research without clear justification, safeguards, and permissions.
  • Data minimisation: Researchers and data custodians should always ask whether identifiable data is truly necessary, or whether anonymous or aggregated data would suffice.
  • Professional responsibility and integrity: Both Ricardo and Beth have duties to act within their roles, follow ethical and legal guidelines, and place the rights and interests of data subjects ahead of convenience.
