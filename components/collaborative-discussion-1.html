Collaborative Discussion 1

Units 1–3 · e-Portfolio Component · Discussion Topic: Codes of Ethics and Professional Conduct

Activity Description

This discussion ran across Units 1–3 and asked us to analyse a professional ethics case study using the ACM and BCS Codes of Ethics and relevant legislation. The focus was on how legal, social, cultural and ethical issues affect computing professionals, with an emphasis on accessibility, discrimination and professional responsibility.

The initial post (minimum 200 words) introduced my analysis of the case; I then responded to peers’ posts and finally wrote a summary post reflecting on how the discussion had shaped my understanding of ethical and professional practice.

Learning Outcome Addressed

  • Appraise the professional, legal, social, cultural and ethical issues that affect computing professionals.

My Contributions

Initial Post – Accessibility in Software Development

In my initial post I discussed the ACM case study Accessibility in Software Development, which describes how a project management tool introduced an inline editing feature that effectively locked out blind users and those with limited hand movement. I argued that by prioritising deadlines over accessibility, the team breached core ACM principles to advance human welfare (1.1) and avoid harm (1.2), and failed to meet their duty under the BCS Code of Conduct to promote equal access and avoid discrimination.

I linked these professional codes to legal frameworks such as the UK Equality Act 2010 and the US Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), highlighting that inaccessible digital systems can amount to unlawful discrimination. I also drew on academic work which describes accessibility as “a core marker of professional quality” and emphasised that inclusive design should be built into projects from the very beginning rather than treated as a late compliance check.

The ACM case study Accessibility in Software Development shows ethical problems that appear when software teams ignore accessibility. A project management tool added an inline editing feature that locked out users who were blind or had limited hand movement. The ACM Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct states that computing professionals must help society and human welfare (Principle 1.1) and must not cause harm (Principle 1.2). Because the team placed deadlines above accessibility, they violated those duties – users were excluded and the organisation’s reputation was damaged.

The BCS Code of Conduct requires members to support equal access to technology and avoid discrimination against people with disabilities. Under UK and US law, failure to provide accessibility can breach the Equality Act 2010 or the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA). Socially, ignoring accessibility pushes people with disabilities to the margins and conflicts with the ethical goal of fair, inclusive technology.

I concluded that accessibility is not an optional enhancement but a professional obligation. Including inclusive design from day one signals professionalism and integrity and helps ensure that technology serves every user.

Peer Responses – Engaging with Different Case Studies

I wrote three peer responses, each engaging with a different ethics case study chosen by my colleagues. Together these responses helped me connect accessibility with broader issues of professional responsibility in computing.

Response to Nelson – AllTogether Accessibility Case

Nelson’s post also analysed the AllTogether accessibility case. In my response I reinforced the idea that accessibility is both a moral responsibility and a strategic necessity. Drawing on Mäkipää and Naarmala, I argued that integrating accessibility from the design phase shows “proactive ethics” and supports universal usability. I agreed that neglecting accessibility damages organisational reputation as well as user trust.

Response to Jaco – Medical Implant Risk Analysis

Jaco discussed a medical implant device case. I supported his critique of “negligible harm” assumptions and linked them to literature showing how small software oversights can escalate into life-threatening failures. I expanded on the need for transparency and explainability in digital health systems, noting that hidden hard-coded logic undermines patient autonomy and trust. I also highlighted that cost savings must never override rigorous risk analysis in high-stakes technologies.

Response to Nikolaos – Blocker Plus Content Filter

Nikolaos explored the Blocker Plus content-filtering system. My response focused on the risks of biased training data and the importance of fairness, accountability and transparency in machine-learning systems. I connected his analysis to current AI governance debates and the EU AI Act’s requirements for documentation, monitoring and human oversight, arguing that ethical responsibility must be backed by enforceable regulation.

Summary Post – What I Learned from the Discussion

In my summary post I reflected that accessibility failures are not just technical bugs but ethical and professional failures. Re-reading my own post and peers’ feedback helped me see that harm arises when teams treat accessibility as optional rather than as a core duty. The discussion introduced me to the idea of “systemic exclusion” – how normal development routines can unintentionally shut people out when accessibility has low priority.

I also concluded that accessibility work signals professional quality. Research we discussed argues that accessibility should start early and continue throughout the project, and that universal usability benefits all users, not only those with disabilities. For me, the key takeaway was that responsible engineering means weaving accessibility into the project from day one and building a culture where anyone can raise an accessibility concern without hesitation.

The discussion on the Accessibility in Software Development case has shown that accessibility failures are not only technical errors but also ethical and professional failures. When AllTogether released an inaccessible feature, the team broke ACM and BCS principles regarding human welfare, harm and equal access. Peer feedback emphasised that harm occurred because accessibility was treated as optional and that this created systemic exclusion.

Peers also highlighted that accessibility is a marker of professional quality and should be integrated from the start of a project. Drawing on Mäkipää and Naarmala and Shneiderman’s work on universal usability, I concluded that accessibility is a legal requirement, a professional obligation and a foundation for an inclusive digital culture.

Personal Reflection

This component helped me move from viewing accessibility as a checklist item to recognising it as a central part of professional identity in computing. Comparing different case studies (accessibility, medical devices and content filtering) showed that the same ethical themes recur: responsibility to avoid harm, the need for inclusive design, and the importance of transparency and accountability.

Engaging with my peers’ posts also developed my academic communication skills. I had to use evidence from professional codes and scholarly sources while responding respectfully and constructively. This experience will directly influence my research project, where I intend to design and evaluate solutions with accessibility and ethical impact embedded from the outset.
